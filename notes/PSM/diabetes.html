🍬 Diabetes Mellitus

Definition

Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by persistent elevation of blood glucose due to defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both.

Exam Definition Diabetes Mellitus is a group of metabolic disorders resulting in chronic hyperglycaemia with disturbances of carbohydrate, fat and protein metabolism.

Classification of Diabetes

Type Description
Type 1 Diabetes Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells causing absolute insulin deficiency.
Type 2 Diabetes Combination of insulin resistance and inadequate insulin secretion.
Gestational Diabetes Diabetes first recognized during pregnancy.
Other Specific Types Genetic defects, pancreatic diseases, endocrine disorders and drug-induced diabetes.

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

  • Usually begins in childhood or adolescence.
  • Autoimmune destruction of beta cells.
  • Absolute insulin deficiency.
  • Requires lifelong insulin therapy.
  • Prone to diabetic ketoacidosis.

Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

  • Most common form of diabetes.
  • Usually develops in adults.
  • Associated with obesity and physical inactivity.
  • Characterized by insulin resistance.
  • Lifestyle modification is an important part of treatment.

Diagnosis

Diagnosis of Diabetes Mellitus is based on clinical features together with laboratory confirmation of elevated blood glucose using standard diagnostic tests.

Risk Factors / Symptoms ↓ Blood Glucose Testing ↓ Confirm Diagnosis ↓ Classify Diabetes ↓ Start Treatment & Lifestyle Modification

Diagnostic Criteria

Test Diagnostic Value
Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L)
2-Hour Plasma Glucose (OGTT) ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L)
HbA1c ≥6.5%
Random Plasma Glucose ≥200 mg/dL with classic symptoms
High Yield Diagnosis should ideally be confirmed by repeat testing unless the patient has unequivocal hyperglycaemia with classic symptoms.

Screening

  • Adults with overweight or obesity and additional risk factors.
  • Adults aged 35 years and above.
  • Individuals with family history of diabetes.
  • Women with previous gestational diabetes.
  • Patients with hypertension or dyslipidaemia.
  • People with sedentary lifestyle.

Clinical Features

  • Increased thirst (Polydipsia)
  • Frequent urination (Polyuria)
  • Increased hunger (Polyphagia)
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Delayed wound healing
  • Recurrent infections

Prevention and Control

Health Education ↓ Healthy Lifestyle ↓ Early Screening ↓ Early Diagnosis ↓ Treatment ↓ Regular Follow-up ↓ Prevent Complications

Diabetes prevention and control require lifestyle modification, regular screening of high-risk individuals, early diagnosis, good glycaemic control and long-term follow-up.

Lifestyle Modification

  • Maintain healthy body weight.
  • Exercise regularly.
  • Stop tobacco use.
  • Limit alcohol intake.
  • Reduce stress.
  • Take medicines regularly.
  • Attend regular follow-up visits.

Physical Activity

  • Regular aerobic exercise.
  • Strength training as appropriate.
  • Avoid prolonged sitting.
  • Increase daily physical activity.
  • Exercise improves insulin sensitivity.

NPCDCS and Diabetes

Under the National Programme for Prevention and Control of Non-Communicable Diseases, diabetes is addressed through health promotion, screening, early diagnosis, treatment and regular follow-up.

  • Population-based screening for eligible adults.
  • Blood glucose measurement.
  • Lifestyle counselling.
  • Referral of high-risk patients.
  • Regular monitoring and follow-up.

Health Education

  • Recognize symptoms early.
  • Maintain healthy eating habits.
  • Exercise daily.
  • Check blood glucose regularly.
  • Foot care.
  • Eye examination.
  • Medication adherence.
  • Avoid tobacco.
